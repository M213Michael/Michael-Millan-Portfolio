01

Methodology

Every parameter in this design was determined through a coarse-to-fine parametric sweep rather than adopted from rule-of-thumb or existing aftermarket designs. The process repeated for each subsystem: broad design study to find the optimum neighborhood, then fine sweep to lock in the exact value, then validation under combined loading.

Step 01
Coarse Sweep
Broad parameter range in large increments to identify the feasible region. Eliminates most of the design space quickly before investing compute time.
Step 02
Export + Pivot
SolidWorks design study results exported to Excel. Pivot tables used to visualize the multi-variable output and identify the optimum neighborhood.
Step 03
Fine Sweep
Narrow range, small steps, centered on the coarse optimum. Confirms the result and quantifies the sensitivity of each parameter.
Step 04
Combined Validation
Final design re-validated under combined realistic loading — simultaneous bump, droop, and steering input — not just isolated worst-case corners.

Why a Spherical Bearing

The outboard LCA joint must accommodate simultaneous camber change through bump and droop, caster change under steering input, and axial plunge from the CV shaft. No bushing geometry can handle all three without inducing bind. A spherical bearing with sufficient misalignment rating is the only joint that allows these motions without transmitting reaction loads back through the arm.

The Misalignment Problem

Misalignment angle is not fixed — it changes continuously as the suspension travels and steers. The bearing must stay below its rated limit at every combination of travel and steering input. With an extended LCA and ±9" of travel, achieving this required optimizing the bearing's axis orientation, not just selecting a bearing with a large enough rating.

The camber change through travel is asymmetric: the knuckle approaches near-vertical at full droop but carries significant camber angle at full bump. This asymmetry means a centered axis orientation wastes margin at one extreme while pressing against the limit at the other.

Key Finding

Axis 2 (front view) was far more sensitive than Axis 1 (side view). Nearly all optimization gain came from moving Axis 2 from 65° to 58°. Caster change through travel is small, so Axis 1 has minimal leverage on peak misalignment. Camber change is large and asymmetric, making Axis 2 the dominant variable.

What Bump Steer Is

Bump steer occurs when the wheel toes in or out as the suspension travels, with no steering input from the driver. The cause is a mismatch between the arc traced by the tie rod's outboard pickup and the arc traced by the LCA's outboard end. If these arcs diverge through travel, the knuckle is forced to rotate — steering the wheel involuntarily.

Why Extension Makes It Worse

Extending the LCA outboard by 3" moved the wheel centerline and the tie rod's outboard pickup with it. The inboard pickup — the steering rack — remained in its stock location. This changed the ratio of tie rod length to LCA length, disrupting the arc relationship that existed in the stock geometry and requiring a new correction study on the extended configuration. The bump steer curve shifted from a near-linear stock shape to a parabolic curve that converges just above ride height.

The Fix

Dropping the steering rack by 0.125" shifts the arc traced by the tie rod so it runs parallel to the LCA through the functional travel range. Parallel arcs produce no bump steer. The singularity at −9" (full droop) is irrelevant in practice — the wheel is unloaded and no meaningful steering force is being transmitted at that extreme.

The Two Constraints

Selecting the lower mount position required balancing two competing constraints: a 12" predetermined shock stroke limit, and packaging clearance with the upper control arm. Moving the mount outboard along the LCA increases the motion ratio — the shock displaces more per inch of wheel travel, which allows softer springs. But it also forces the shock body to sweep further inward toward the UCA as the LCA rotates into bump.

Why Higher MR Was Rejected

A higher percentage mount position produces a higher average motion ratio, which is geometrically desirable — it allows softer spring rates and more damping leverage. But it also requires more shock stroke to cover the same wheel travel, and it causes the shock body to sweep further into the UCA at full bump. At 58%, the shock body clears the UCA through the full travel range with the upper eye at Z=11.5". Any higher percentage makes that clearance impossible without redesigning the UCA into a U-shape.

The bump margin of 0.5" is tighter than droop by design. A hydraulic bump stop will be installed to progressively resist the last inch of compression, so the coilover never reaches its mechanical limit under normal use. The tight bump margin is intentional, not a shortfall.

Rising Rate Behavior

At the 58% position the motion ratio is not constant — it rises from ride height into full bump. This is a favorable characteristic: the suspension progressively stiffens as it compresses, providing a natural resistance to bottoming out that complements the hydraulic bump stop rather than relying on it entirely.

Three-Axis Study

The upper eye position was optimized across three axes. The X component (fore-aft) was constrained by the lower eye: both eyes were aligned to the same sweep plane so the shock shaft sees no out-of-plane bending as the suspension travels. The Y component (vertical) was set to achieve the correct eye-to-eye distance within the Fox stroke limits. The Z component (lateral, inboard/outboard) was swept to maximize angle progression toward vertical through bump while maintaining clearance with the UCA.

The UCA Packaging Constraint

Moving the upper eye further outboard in Z forces the UCA tubes to route around the shock body rather than converging directly on the outboard spherical bearing. At Z=11.5", the UCA still forms a near-triangular shape with adequate tube geometry. Past Z=11.5", the UCA tubes would have to extend outboard and then come back inboard to reach the bearing — deforming the arm into a U-shape that is structurally and geometrically impractical. Z=11.5" is therefore a design boundary, not a hard mechanical interference.

Why the U-Curve is Acceptable

The shock angle decreases from droop to just past ride height, then recovers toward vertical through full bump. This U-shape is a consequence of the UCA packaging constraint. The rising angle from ride height into bump is where it matters most — the suspension stiffens progressively through the region it uses hardest. The droop region is addressed by the dual-rate spring system.

Dual-Rate Spring Compensation

In the droop region, the falling shock angle reduces the effective spring rate felt at the wheel. Without compensation, the suspension would have insufficient resistance to wheel droop — the wheel could unload from the ground under low forces, reducing traction and control. The tender spring maintains a baseline spring force through the droop range, keeping the wheel loaded against the ground until the main spring takes over. The main spring then handles the larger compression events where the geometric rising rate is working in its favor.

3.0 Over 2.5

The 3.0 body was chosen over the 2.5 for its substantially larger oil volume and thermal capacity. This project includes documented transmission overheating during sustained dune use — thermal management under high-intensity cycling was a real design requirement, not a theoretical concern. The larger body also provides greater damping range, a stronger 1" shaft versus 7/8", and costs only ~$150 more. The performance increase justified the marginal cost difference.

Internal Bypass with DSC

The DSC adjuster provides velocity-sensitive damping tunability — the shock responds differently to slow trail inputs versus sharp high-speed hits — without the complexity and cost of an external bypass system. It occupies a practical middle ground between a standard reservoir shock and a full external bypass setup.

12" Stroke — Sized for the Future

The current travel limit is ±7", imposed by the stock knuckle's vertical UCA bolt orientation, which forces all travel through bearing misalignment rather than rotation. The UCA spherical bearing reaches its misalignment limit at ±7", binding the FMZ adapter against the knuckle. A fabricated knuckle with a horizontal UCA bolt orientation will convert this travel to bearing rotation, unlocking the full ±9" the geometry was designed for. The 12" stroke was selected for that future state — not for the current ±7" limitation — so the coilover remains valid when the knuckle upgrade is completed, avoiding a second shock purchase.
